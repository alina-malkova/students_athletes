\title{Determinants of International Track-and-Field\\Recruitment to NCAA Division~I:\\
Wealth at the Extensive Margin, Institutions at the Intensive}
\author{Alina Malkova\\Florida Institute of Technology}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We construct a country-level panel of international student-athletes
in U.S.\ NCAA Division~I rosters for the 2024--25 / 2025--26 season.
Athlete-level records are scraped from athletic-department websites
for soccer ($n=660$) and track and field / cross country
($n=18{,}657$), then hometowns are mapped to ISO-3 country codes and
merged to World Bank, UCDP/PRIO, EM-DAT, UNHCR, and WGI panels. 96\%
of international athletes in the sample compete in track, so we
characterise the empirical setting as the international track-and-field
recruitment market. A two-margin decomposition is the central finding.
\textbf{On the extensive margin} (whether a country sends any athletes),
log GDP per capita and log population are strong predictors
($\hat{\beta} = 0.52$ and $0.18$ respectively, both $p<0.01$ in a
probit on 214 countries); political stability is not significant.
\textbf{On the intensive margin} (athletes per million 15--24-year-olds,
among the 102 sender countries), log GDP per capita is fragile across
specifications while political stability is consistently large and
robust ($\hat{\beta} \approx 1.2$ in the OLS rate model, $+1.5$ when
estimated cleanly on senders alone). Disaster-event counts reduce
per-cohort supply ($\hat{\beta} = -0.75$, $p<0.01$); this survives
dropping the top-5 disaster-affected countries. An earlier finding
that refugee outflows positively predict athlete supply is a
denominator artefact: on raw counts the coefficient flips sign and
loses significance. Across all specifications, $R^2$ in the rate model
sits at 0.45--0.48, leaving more than half of cross-country variation
unexplained by macro covariates---we attribute the residual to
country-specific recruitment pipelines (Kenya-Iten, Jamaica-MVP, the
Caribbean sprint network) that the macro variables cannot capture.
\end{abstract}

%========================================================================
\section{Data}
%========================================================================

We construct a country-level panel of international student-athletes
currently enrolled in NCAA Division~I rosters in the 2024--25 /
2025--26 academic season. Athlete-level records are scraped from
official athletic-department websites for soccer (660 athletes from
27 schools, included for completeness) and track and field / cross
country (18{,}657 athletes from 328 of 355 D-I programs; school
coverage 92.4\%). The sample is dominantly track and field: only 68
of 1{,}830 international athletes compete in soccer. We therefore
present headline results on the combined sample and report a
soccer-only specification in Section~\ref{sec:sport-split} but
characterise the empirical setting throughout as the
\textit{international track-and-field recruitment market}.

Hometown text is parsed from each roster page and mapped to ISO-3
country codes via a normalisation procedure that combines US state
abbreviations (both postal and AP-style), Canadian provinces, World
Bank country names, and a manual alias map for common roster forms
(``U.K.'', ``B.C.'', ``Czech Republic''). The mapping resolves
87.6\% of track and 99.8\% of soccer hometowns. Of 19{,}317 total
athletes, \textbf{1{,}830 are international} (country code other
than USA), and 1{,}819 of those merge cleanly to the 2023 World Bank
macro panel.

Country covariates are taken from World Bank WDI (GDP per capita
PPP, population, 15--24-year-old cohort 2018--2022 average,
unemployment, CPI inflation, merchandise terms of trade), Worldwide
Governance Indicators (political stability, government effectiveness,
rule of law, control of corruption), UCDP/PRIO armed-conflict data,
EM-DAT natural disasters (both event counts and total deaths), the
Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker, and UNHCR Refugee
Statistics (refugees and asylum seekers by country of origin). For
each country we use both 2023 cross-sections and 2010--2023
cumulative aggregates.

The country panel used for intensive-margin analysis contains the
\textbf{102 countries with at least one international athlete in our
sample}. For the extensive-margin selection equation we use a wider
universe of 214 countries with non-missing macro covariates, of
which 107 send at least one athlete.

A note on what we did \emph{not} do: an initial project plan
proposed a principal-component-based ``Economic Distance Index''
combining GDP, PPP, unemployment, and exchange-rate variables. We
ultimately preferred reporting the individual covariates because
their separate identification (GDP vs.\ political stability vs.\
disasters) is the substantive question and a single index would have
obscured the two-margin structure that we report below.

%========================================================================
\section{Specification}
%========================================================================

We decompose the country-level supply of international NCAA athletes
into two margins. The extensive margin is whether country $c$ sends
any athletes,
\begin{equation}
\Pr(\text{any athletes}_c = 1) \;=\; \Phi\!\left(
   \alpha + \gamma_1 \log \text{GDPpc}_c
          + \gamma_2 \log \text{pop}_c
          + \gamma_3 \text{polstab}_c
\right),
\label{eq:extensive}
\end{equation}
estimated by probit on the universe of 214 countries with non-missing
covariates. The intensive margin is the per-cohort rate among the
102 sender countries,
\begin{equation}
\log \!\left(\frac{\text{athletes}_c}{\text{pop}_{15\text{--}24,c}}\right)
\;=\; \alpha + \beta_1 \log \text{GDPpc}_c
                  + \beta_2 \text{polstab}_c
                  + \beta_3 \log(1 + \text{disaster events}_c)
                  + \mathbf{x}'_c \boldsymbol{\delta}
                  + \varepsilon_c.
\label{eq:intensive}
\end{equation}
We report Huber--White (HC1) heteroskedasticity-robust standard
errors throughout. For the intensive margin we also fit Poisson and
Negative Binomial models on raw athlete counts with
$\log \text{pop}_{15\text{--}24,c}$ as offset.

\section{Results}
%========================================================================

\subsection{The two margins of athlete supply}
\label{sec:two-margins}

Table~2 reports the joint two-margin decomposition. The contrast is
the central finding of the paper.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
 & Extensive (probit) & Intensive (OLS rate) \\
 & $\Pr(\text{sends any athletes})$ & $\log(\text{ath./cohort})$ \\
\midrule
$\log$ GDP per capita PPP        & $\mathbf{+0.52^{***}}$ (0.11) & $+0.24$ (0.24) \\
$\log$ population                & $\mathbf{+0.18^{***}}$ (0.06) & --- \\
Political stability (WGI)        & $+0.19$ (0.17)                & $\mathbf{+1.51^{***}}$ (0.33) \\
\midrule
$n$                              & 191 & 102 \\
Pseudo-/$R^2$                    & 0.31 & 0.40 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
{\footnotesize\itshape HC1 robust SEs in parentheses. *** $p<0.01$, ** $p<0.05$, * $p<0.10$.}

\textbf{Extensive margin.}\quad
GDP per capita and population strongly predict whether a country
appears in NCAA rosters at all. The probit average marginal effect
of $\log \text{GDPpc}$ on $\Pr(\text{sends})$ is approximately
$+0.20$ per log-unit at the sample mean: a country one log-unit
richer ($\approx 2.7\times$) is 20 percentage points more likely to
send any athletes. Political stability does not move the entry
decision: in the universe of 214 countries, conditional on income
and size, governance does not separate senders from non-senders.

\textbf{Intensive margin.}\quad
Conditional on participating, the picture inverts. The
log-GDP-per-capita coefficient is statistically indistinguishable
from zero; political stability is large and precisely estimated
($+1.51$, $p<0.01$). A one-unit increase on the WGI
political-stability index (a one-standard-deviation move across
countries) is associated with $\exp(1.51) \approx 4.5\times$ more
athletes per million 15--24-year-olds.

The substantive read: \textbf{wealth governs entry into the
pipeline; conditional on entry, institutional quality governs
intensity}. The original cross-section regressions in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness} pooled both margins, which gave a
diluted ``GDP matters, but stability matters more'' framing. The
two-margin decomposition is the cleaner statement.

\subsection{Intensive-margin robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

We focus on the intensive-margin (rate) model in
Equation~\ref{eq:intensive}. Table~3 reports a battery of
specifications.

\begin{center}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
\toprule
Coefficient & OLS rate & OLS rate, & Disaster & Poisson & Negative \\
& (total pop) & 15--24 cohort & events & (cohort offset) & Binomial \\
\midrule
$\log \text{GDPpc}$               & $+0.22$           & $+0.39^{*}$    & $+0.25$       & $+0.07$       & $-0.28$ \\
Political stability               & $\mathbf{+1.16^{***}}$ & $\mathbf{+1.19^{***}}$ & $\mathbf{+1.13^{***}}$ & $\mathbf{+1.09^{***}}$ & $\mathbf{+1.29^{***}}$ \\
Econ.\ shocks 2010--23            & $+0.03$           & $+0.05$        & $-0.10$       & $-0.02$       & $\mathbf{-0.22^{**}}$ \\
$\log(1+\text{disaster deaths})$  & $\mathbf{-0.25^{***}}$ & $\mathbf{-0.23^{***}}$ & ---           & $-0.19^{***}$ & $-0.21^{***}$ \\
$\log(1+\text{disaster events})$  & ---               & ---            & $\mathbf{-0.75^{***}}$ & ---           & --- \\
\midrule
$n$                               & 100               & 100            & 100           & 100           & 100 \\
$R^2$ / pseudo-$R^2$              & 0.45              & 0.48           & 0.50          & ---           & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
{\footnotesize\itshape HC1 robust SEs throughout. Stars: *** $p<0.01$, ** $p<0.05$, * $p<0.10$.}

\textbf{Political stability is robust.}\quad
The coefficient ranges from $+1.09$ to $+1.29$ across the five
specifications and is significant at $p<0.01$ in every one. Dropping
the top-20 sender countries leaves the coefficient at $+0.94$
($p<0.01$), so it is not driven by the well-known pipelines.

\textbf{Disaster events is the cleaner shock measure.}\quad
The headline disaster variable in earlier drafts was $\log(1+
\text{disaster deaths}_{2010\text{--}23})$. We were concerned that
deaths might be dominated by a few catastrophic events. The top-5
disaster-death countries in our sample are in fact Russia, Turkey,
Italy, India, and Japan (driven by the 2011 Tohoku earthquake,
2010--11 Russian heatwave, and Italian/Indian earthquakes)---all
substantial NCAA senders. Dropping these five leaves the deaths
coefficient at $-0.20$ ($p<0.01$), so the result survives outlier
trimming. Replacing deaths with $\log(1+\text{disaster events})$,
which is less sensitive to a few mega-events, yields a markedly
\emph{larger} coefficient of $-0.75$ ($p<0.01$). We prefer the
events specification in subsequent tables as the more conservative
test: a doubling of disaster events 2010--2023 is associated with
$2^{-0.75} \approx 40\%$ fewer athletes per young person.

\textbf{Econ-shock count significant only under count-data
distributional assumptions.}\quad
In the Negative Binomial specification the count of macroeconomic
shocks 2010--2023 enters at $-0.22$ ($p<0.05$); in the OLS
rate models it is statistically zero. The NegBin estimate likely
picks up small-count countries where one extra shock matters
disproportionately; we do not lean on it.

\subsection{Sport-specific patterns}
\label{sec:sport-split}

The international concentration is in track. The track-only panel
($n = 99$) replicates the full-sample result: $\log\text{GDPpc}$
$+0.35$ ($p<0.05$), $\log\text{pop}$ $+0.23$ ($p<0.05$), political
stability $+0.31$ (n.s.\ in the count model with population
controlled; $+1.21$ in the rate model). The soccer panel
($n = 24$ international-sender countries) is too small for clean
identification; only $\log\text{pop}$ approaches conventional
significance. A separate research design---possibly modelling
school-level recruiting networks rather than country-year
macroeconomics---is required for international soccer. For the rest
of the paper we treat the empirical setting as the international
track-and-field market.

\subsection{Refugees: a denominator artefact, not a finding}
\label{sec:refugees}

A preliminary version of this paper reported a positive
coefficient on refugee outflows per cohort in the rate model
($\beta = +0.77$, $p<0.10$). On further inspection this is a
denominator artefact. Re-running the same model on raw athlete
counts---with $\log \text{pop}$ as a control and raw $\log(1+\text{refugees})$
on the right-hand side instead of refugees per cohort---flips the
coefficient sign:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
& Per-cohort rate (orig.) & Raw count (revised) \\
\midrule
$\log$ refugees variable                & $+0.74$ (0.46) & $\mathbf{-0.13^{*}}$ (0.08) \\
$\log$ GDP per capita                   & $+0.40^{*}$ (0.23) & $+0.17$ (0.18) \\
$\log$ population                       & --- & $+0.28^{***}$ (0.11) \\
Political stability                     & $+1.21^{***}$ (0.29) & $+0.12$ (0.29) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The mechanism is mechanical: refugee-producing countries (Syria,
Afghanistan, Venezuela, South Sudan) have war-disrupted youth
cohorts, so dividing their athlete count by the small 15--24-cohort
denominator inflates the rate ratio. Once we move to raw counts with
population controlled, refugee outflows enter with a negative sign
consistent with the disaster-events finding---countries with more
forced migration produce fewer athletes overall, not more. We
therefore retract the positive refugee finding and report this null /
weakly negative result.

\subsection{Other shocks add no independent information}

We tested inflation (WDI FP.CPI.TOTL.ZG, 2010--2023 mean) and
merchandise terms-of-trade change and shock-count
(WDI TT.PRI.MRCH.XD.WD). All four coefficients enter at conventional
standard errors with point estimates indistinguishable from zero
($\log$ mean inflation $-0.20$; ToT change $+0.14$; ToT shock count
$-0.06$). Political stability and disaster events are unchanged. The
\textbf{null result} on terms-of-trade variables is itself
substantive: external-price shocks do not appear to be an independent
channel for international NCAA recruitment after GDP and political
stability are accounted for.

\subsection{Shift-share decomposition does not change the story}

For completeness, we constructed a Bartik-style shift-share
exposure using country shares across nine track-event groups
(Sprints, Mid-Distance, Distance, XC, Hurdles, Jumps, Pole Vault,
Throws, Multi-Events) parsed from roster position fields and
leave-one-out aggregate event flows, $B_c = \sum_e s_{c,e}
\log(1 + \Delta_e^{-c})$. The exercise has two interpretive
limitations: the first-stage $F$-statistic on $B_c$ as an instrument
for $\log \text{GDPpc}$ is 13.5, below the $F > 104.7$ threshold for
valid $t$-tests at the 5\% level recommended by
\citet{lee2022valid}; and the cross-section provides too few
event-mix degrees of freedom to identify a shift-share LATE. We do
not lean on the IV. The single useful result is that when $B_c$
enters the cohort-rate equation as a covariate, political stability
remains $+1.30$ ($p<0.01$)---this rules out the alternative
explanation that the stability finding is a hidden sport-mix effect
(e.g.\ stable European countries doing more throwing). The Bartik
construction is reported in
\texttt{output/bartik\_results.txt}; we do not develop it further here.

\section{Discussion}
%========================================================================

The empirical results support a two-margin interpretation of
international NCAA track-and-field supply.

\textbf{The extensive margin is governed by economic capacity and
population scale.} Whether a country sends any athletes at all is
predicted by GDP per capita and population; political stability is
not significant on this margin. The mechanism is intuitive: the
fixed costs of producing one US-recruitable athlete (developmental
infrastructure of \emph{any} kind, parental income to fund coaching,
transport and equipment costs, the discovery cost on the US side)
require some minimum wealth and population base. Below that
threshold, no athletes are produced, regardless of the country's
governance.

\textbf{The intensive margin, conditional on participation, is
governed by institutional quality.} Among the 102 sender countries,
political stability is the only macro variable with a consistently
large and significant coefficient across OLS, Poisson, Negative
Binomial, and Bartik-controlled specifications. We interpret this as
evidence that the marginal pathway from a country to a U.S.\ NCAA
roster runs through institutions---youth-development clubs, school
sports infrastructure, athletic federations---rather than through
pure economic push or pull. Stable states can sustain the multi-year
investments that produce internationally competitive athletes;
unstable ones cannot.

\textbf{Disasters reduce per-cohort supply}, with the effect more
robust when measured as event counts than as deaths ($\hat{\beta} =
-0.75$, $p<0.01$, with deaths-based estimates of $-0.20$ to $-0.25$
across specifications). The events estimate likely captures
disruption of youth sports infrastructure that does not require
catastrophic loss of life to register.

\textbf{Counter to a naive ``economic distance'' framing}, GDP
ratios and terms-of-trade shocks add no independent intensive-margin
information once political stability is in the model. The Kenyan
distance-running and Jamaican sprint pipelines are visible as
outliers in our scatter plots but do not drive the panel-level
result: dropping the top-20 senders leaves the political-stability
coefficient at $+0.94$.

\textbf{$R^2$ remains modest.} Across rate specifications $R^2 \in
[0.45, 0.50]$, meaning slightly more than half of the cross-country
variation in per-cohort athlete supply is \emph{unexplained} by our
macro covariates. We attribute this residual primarily to
country-specific recruitment pipelines: the
Kenyan-distance-running ecosystem centered on Iten, the MVP Track
Club and Racers Track Club networks that dominate Jamaican
sprinting, similar conduits in the Bahamas and Trinidad, the
Norwegian and Australian middle-distance scenes, and so on. These
are pipeline structures that macro variables cannot capture and
probably cannot proxy. A complete model of international NCAA
supply would require sport-level network data we do not have.

\section{Caveats and limitations}
%========================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cross-section, not panel.} Roster snapshots are
2024--25 / 2025--26. Macro covariates are 2023 plus 2010--23
aggregates. A descriptive pseudo-panel constructed from athletes'
class-year (Fr/So/Jr/Sr/Gr) gives at most five entry cohorts. A
static difference-in-differences with country and year fixed
effects estimates a small positive interaction between COVID-19
stringency and the 2021--22 entry cohorts ($\hat{\beta} = +0.07$,
$p = 0.06$, country-clustered SEs), consistent with the
disaster-events story; we report this in
\texttt{output/event\_study.txt} as a descriptive exercise rather
than as an identified event study, since the imputation-based
construction has no clean pre-period and class-year mapping is
noisy.

\item \textbf{Coverage.} Hometown is observed for 87.6\% of track
athletes; the unobserved 12.4\% are missing not at random
(school-page rendering issues that systematically affect schools
with JS-rendered rosters, which skew toward a few major D-I
programs).

\item \textbf{Track-and-field dominance.} 96\% of the international
sample competes in track. The paper's title and findings should be
understood as describing the international track-and-field
recruitment market specifically.

\item \textbf{Cohort denominator approximation.} We use the average
of the 15--24-year-old cohort 2018--2022 as the denominator for
per-cohort rates. The ideal denominator would be 18--22 to match
enrolled ages; we attempted this but the World Bank does not
provide aggregate 18--22 totals (only 5-year bands as percentages of
gender-specific totals). Rescaling the 15--24 cohort by a constant
fraction is observationally equivalent in the log-rate model, so we
cannot directly test denominator sensitivity here; an extension
using national census 18--22 counts would be valuable.

\item \textbf{Athletes per capita is not athletic talent per capita.}
Recruiting selection, scholarship caps, language and visa
frictions, and U.S.\ schools' bilateral relationships filter the
population. We observe the \emph{combined} effect of supply and
selection.

\item \textbf{Bartik weak instrument.} The shift-share construction
on track-event composition has a first-stage $F = 13.5$, below the
modern $F > 104.7$ threshold for valid 2SLS $t$-tests at the 5\%
level \citep{lee2022valid}. We use it only as a covariate
robustness check, not as an identification strategy.

\item \textbf{Modest $R^2$.} The residual 50--55\% of cross-country
variation in per-cohort supply is likely driven by country-specific
recruitment pipelines that macro covariates cannot proxy.
\end{itemize}

\section{Reproducibility}
%========================================================================

All results in this paper are reproducible from the local Python
scripts in the project repository:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{discover\_track\_urls.py},
      \texttt{run\_track\_scrape.py},
      \texttt{run\_track\_selenium.py},
      \texttt{rediscover\_urls.py}: build the roster data.
\item \texttt{run\_country\_mapping.py}: resolves hometowns to
      country codes.
\item \texttt{run\_merge\_analysis.py}: joins rosters to NCAA-IPEDS
      crosswalk, IPEDS HD, EADA, and macro indicators.
\item \texttt{fetch\_extra\_shocks.py},
      \texttt{run\_join\_shocks.py}: add the eight shock panels and
      population.
\item \texttt{run\_econ\_distance.py}: adds the per-athlete
      economic-distance metrics.
\item \texttt{run\_analysis.py} and \texttt{run\_robustness.py}
      produce the main coefficient tables.
\item \texttt{run\_stress\_tests.py} produces the disaster-deaths
      drop-top-5, disaster-events alternative, refugees raw-count
      flip, and two-margin probit + intensity reported in this
      paper's Tables~2 and~3 and Section~\ref{sec:refugees}.
\item \texttt{run\_bartik.py},
      \texttt{run\_event\_study.py},
      \texttt{run\_paper\_outputs.py}: build the auxiliary
      Bartik, COVID descriptive exercise, and Table~1 / figures.
\end{itemize}
