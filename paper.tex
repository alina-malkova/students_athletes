\title{Two Margins of International Track-and-Field\\Recruitment to NCAA Division~I:\\
Wealth, Population, and the Limits of Macro Explanation}
\author{Alina Malkova\\Florida Institute of Technology}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We construct a country-level panel of international student-athletes
in U.S.\ NCAA Division~I rosters for the 2024--25 / 2025--26 season.
Athlete-level records are scraped from athletic-department websites
for soccer ($n=660$) and track and field / cross country
($n=18{,}657$); 96\% of the international sample competes in track,
so we characterise the empirical setting as the international
track-and-field recruitment market. We decompose athlete supply
along two margins. \textbf{On the extensive margin} (whether a country
sends any athletes at all), log GDP per capita and log population are
strong independent predictors ($\hat\beta = 0.52$ and $0.18$, both
$p<0.01$ in a probit on 191 countries); political stability is not.
\textbf{On the intensive margin} (among 102 sender countries), the
elasticity of athletes with respect to the 15--24-year-old cohort is
approximately $0.33$---strongly sublinear---and log GDP per capita is
the only additional robust predictor ($\hat\beta = 0.37$, $p<0.05$).
Political stability, disaster events, conflict years, refugee
outflows, and macroeconomic shocks are statistically indistinguishable
from zero once we estimate the population elasticity freely instead
of imposing the unit-elasticity restriction implicit in
athletes-per-cohort rate models. We document this in Section~3.3:
the rate-model coefficient on political stability is $+1.12$
($p<0.01$) and on disaster events is $-0.74$ ($p<0.01$), but both
collapse once $\log \text{pop}$ enters the equation as a free
regressor. We thus retract earlier framings of this work that
emphasised institutional quality as an intensive-margin driver. The
honest finding is smaller: wealth and population scale govern entry
into the international NCAA pipeline; conditional on entry, wealth
governs intensity with diminishing returns to size, and the macro
covariates we have do not explain the rest. $R^2$ in the count model
is $0.20$--$0.25$, with the residual most likely attributable to
country-specific recruitment pipelines (Kenya--Iten, Jamaica--MVP,
the Caribbean sprint network) that macro variables cannot proxy.
\end{abstract}

%========================================================================
\section{Data}
%========================================================================

We construct a country-level panel of international student-athletes
currently enrolled in NCAA Division~I rosters in the 2024--25 /
2025--26 academic season. Athlete-level records are scraped from
official athletic-department websites for soccer (660 athletes from
27 schools) and track and field / cross country (18{,}657 athletes
from 328 of 355 D-I programs; school coverage 92.4\%). Only 68 of
1{,}830 international athletes compete in soccer, so we report the
combined sample in headline tables but characterise the empirical
setting throughout as the \textit{international track-and-field
recruitment market}; the soccer panel is too small to identify
country-level structural effects on its own.

Hometown text is parsed from each roster page and mapped to ISO-3
country codes via a normalisation procedure that combines US state
abbreviations (both postal and AP-style), Canadian provinces, World
Bank country names, and a manual alias map for common roster forms
(``U.K.'', ``B.C.'', ``Czech Republic''). The mapping resolves
87.6\% of track and 99.8\% of soccer hometowns. Of 19{,}317 total
athletes, \textbf{1{,}830 are international} (country code other
than USA), and 1{,}819 of those merge cleanly to the 2023 World Bank
macro panel.

Country covariates are taken from World Bank WDI (GDP per capita PPP,
population, 15--24-year-old cohort 2018--2022 average, unemployment,
CPI inflation, merchandise terms of trade), Worldwide Governance
Indicators (political stability, government effectiveness, rule of
law, control of corruption), UCDP/PRIO armed-conflict data, EM-DAT
natural disasters (event counts and total deaths), the Oxford
COVID-19 Government Response Tracker, and UNHCR Refugee Statistics
(refugees and asylum seekers by country of origin). For each country
we use both 2023 cross-sections and 2010--2023 cumulative aggregates.
The country panel used for intensive-margin analysis contains the
\textbf{102 countries with at least one international athlete}. The
extensive-margin probit uses a broader universe of 214 countries
with non-missing macro covariates, of which 107 send at least one
athlete.

An initial project plan proposed a principal-component-based
``Economic Distance Index.'' We ultimately preferred reporting the
individual covariates because their separate identification (GDP vs.\
political stability vs.\ disasters vs.\ population scale) is the
substantive question, and---as we show in Section~3.3---imposing
even seemingly innocuous restrictions on the joint covariate
structure (such as the unit population elasticity implicit in a
per-cohort rate outcome) can produce coefficients that look
significant but vanish under freer specifications.

%========================================================================
\section{Specification}
%========================================================================

We decompose international NCAA athlete supply into two margins. The
extensive margin is whether country $c$ sends any athletes,
\begin{equation}
\Pr(\text{any athletes}_c = 1) \;=\; \Phi\!\left(
   \alpha + \gamma_1 \log \text{GDPpc}_c
          + \gamma_2 \log \text{pop}_c
          + \gamma_3 \text{polstab}_c
\right),
\label{eq:extensive}
\end{equation}
estimated by probit on the 191 countries with non-missing covariates
in the full universe.

The intensive margin is the per-country athlete count among the 102
senders. We deliberately use the count outcome with $\log
\text{pop}_{15\text{--}24,c}$ as a free regressor,
\begin{equation}
\log \text{athletes}_c \;=\; \alpha
   + \beta_1 \log \text{pop}_{15\text{--}24,c}
   + \beta_2 \log \text{GDPpc}_c
   + \beta_3 \text{polstab}_c
   + \mathbf{x}_c' \boldsymbol{\delta} + \varepsilon_c,
\label{eq:intensive}
\end{equation}
rather than the per-cohort rate outcome used in the previous draft of
this work. Section~3.3 shows that imposing the unit-elasticity
restriction $\beta_1 = -1$ (the rate-model assumption) is rejected
sharply by the data: the estimated $\hat\beta_1$ in the count model
is approximately $+0.33$, meaning athletes scale roughly as
$\text{pop}^{0.33}$. Imposing $-1$ in the rate model forces this
mis-specification into other coefficients, generating spurious
``effects'' for political stability and disaster events. The
free-elasticity count model is the honest test.

We report Huber--White (HC1) heteroskedasticity-robust standard
errors throughout.

Income and size are strong, independent predictors of whether a

Three things to flag.

\textbf{Athletes scale sublinearly with cohort size.}\quad
The estimated elasticity is $\hat\beta_1 \approx 0.33$ in the base
specification and is stable across columns. A doubling of the 15--24
cohort is associated with $2^{0.33} \approx 1.26 \times$ more
athletes, not $2 \times$. The most plausible mechanism is that the
US recruitment system has finite capacity per country: the marginal
NCAA program does not scale its international intake one-for-one
with the size of any single source country. Small wealthy countries
(Norway, Iceland, the Bahamas, Trinidad) are therefore systematically
over-represented per capita relative to populous ones (India,
Indonesia, Pakistan). This is the dominant intensive-margin fact in
the data.

\textbf{Log GDP per capita is the only other robust predictor.}\quad
The coefficient is $\hat\beta_2 \approx 0.37$ ($p<0.05$) in columns
(1), (2), and (4). It attenuates to $+0.26$ (n.s.) in column (3)
when all four shock variables are entered jointly, which we attribute
to limited statistical power with only 100 observations and seven
right-hand-side variables; the drop-top-20 specification in column
(4) returns $\hat\beta_2$ to $+0.37$ ($p<0.01$), supporting the
interpretation that the column-(3) attenuation is a degrees-of-freedom
artefact rather than substantive.

\textbf{Macro shocks and institutional quality do not add explanatory
power.}\quad
Disaster events, political stability, economic shocks, conflict
years, and refugee outflows are all individually insignificant in
columns (2)--(4). Most have point estimates well within one standard
error of zero. The combined increase in $R^2$ from spec (1) to spec
(3) is only $0.03$. We cannot reject the null that, conditional on
GDP per capita and the sublinear population term, these variables
have no additional intensive-margin effect.

\subsection{Methodological note: the rate-model artefact}
\label{sec:rate-pitfall}

An earlier draft of this paper reported intensive-margin results
using $\log(\text{athletes}/\text{pop}_{15\text{--}24})$ as the
outcome and \emph{omitting} $\log\text{pop}$ from the right-hand
side. That specification implicitly imposes a unit elasticity of
athletes with respect to population:
\begin{equation*}
\log(\text{athletes}/\text{pop}) \;=\; \log(\text{athletes}) - \log(\text{pop}),
\end{equation*}
so a coefficient of zero on the right-hand side of the rate
regression corresponds to $\beta_{\text{pop}} = 1$ in the count
regression. The data reject this restriction: the free-elasticity
estimate is $\hat\beta_{\text{pop}} \approx 0.33$, not $1$. Imposing
the wrong restriction forces the resulting mis-specification error
into the other coefficients. The table below shows the consequence
for the variables that previously appeared as the paper's headline
findings.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
Coefficient & Rate model & Count model & Difference \\
            & (implicit $\beta_{\text{pop}} = 1$) & ($\beta_{\text{pop}}$ free) & \\
\midrule
$\log \text{GDPpc}$                         & $+0.25$  (0.22)   & $\mathbf{+0.37^{**}}$ (0.16)   & --- \\
Political stability                         & $\mathbf{+1.12^{***}}$ (0.27) & $+0.37$ (0.25)                & \textbf{collapses} \\
$\log(1+\text{disaster events})$            & $\mathbf{-0.74^{***}}$ (0.16) & $+0.03$ (0.19)                & \textbf{collapses} \\
$\log\text{pop}_{15\text{--}24}$            & (constrained $= -1$)          & $\mathbf{+0.33^{***}}$ (0.11) & --- \\
\midrule
$R^2$                                       & 0.53                          & 0.21                          & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The rate model's apparent ``political stability dominates'' and
``disaster events suppress supply'' findings are size-effect
artefacts. Small countries tend to be high on the WGI political-
stability scale (Norway, Iceland, Switzerland, New Zealand) and tend
to have few EM-DAT disaster events (a function of country area and
hazard exposure as much as governance), and large countries tend to
have the opposite. When we constrain the population elasticity to
$-1$ in the rate model, this small-country / large-country
correlation gets loaded onto the stability and disaster coefficients
that should have been zero. Freeing the elasticity reveals the
underlying null result.

This pitfall is not specific to our paper; it applies to any
cross-country regression that uses a per-capita or per-cohort
outcome without verifying that the implicit elasticity matches the
data. We document it here because it materially changed the empirical
conclusions of an earlier version of this work, and because the same
mechanism likely affects other applied work in international
recruitment and migration that uses per-capita rates as outcomes.

\subsection{Sport-specific patterns}
\label{sec:sport-split}

The international concentration is in track. The track-only count
panel ($n = 99$) is essentially the headline result: $\log
\text{pop}_{15\text{--}24}$ $+0.32$ ($p<0.01$), $\log \text{GDPpc}$
$+0.37$ ($p<0.05$), political stability $+0.36$ (n.s.). The soccer
panel ($n = 24$) is too small for clean identification: only $\log
\text{pop}$ approaches conventional significance. The paper's
findings should be understood as describing the international
track-and-field market specifically; international soccer recruitment
likely operates through school-level network channels (specific
academies, alumni connections) that a country-year macro design
cannot capture, and we leave that question to future work.

\subsection{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

We test the count-model intensive-margin result against several
alternative specifications.

\textbf{Disaster deaths instead of events.}\quad
The previous draft used $\log(1+\text{disaster deaths})$ rather than
events. The deaths variable in the rate model gives $-0.23$
($p<0.01$); in the count model with $\log \text{pop}_{15\text{--}24}$
freely estimated it collapses to $-0.04$ (n.s.). The deaths effect
was driven by the population-size confound, not by the catastrophic-
event countries (the top-5 disaster-death countries in our sample
are Russia, Turkey, Italy, India, and Japan---all large, all
substantial NCAA senders---rather than the small high-fatality cases
of Haiti, Myanmar, or Nepal that one might naively expect). Events
is the cleaner measure even before the size correction, but neither
survives the size correction.

\textbf{Refugees: counts vs.\ rates.}\quad
The rate model gave a positive marginal coefficient on refugees per
cohort ($+0.74$, n.s. with disasters in the model). On raw counts
with $\log \text{pop}$ controlled, refugee outflows enter at $-0.13$
($p<0.10$). The sign flip is consistent with the war-disrupted-cohort
denominator story: countries with large refugee outflows (Syria,
Afghanistan, Venezuela, South Sudan) have shrinking 15--24 cohorts,
so the rate ratio is inflated independent of any underlying
recruitment effect. In the count model, conditional on size, refugee
outflows do not predict athlete supply.

\textbf{Drop-top-20 senders.}\quad
Dropping the 20 countries with the most international athletes
(column 4 of Table~3) shrinks the sample to 80 countries and the
$R^2$ to 0.15. The GDP per capita coefficient stays at $+0.37$
($p<0.01$); the population elasticity falls to $+0.16$ ($p<0.05$);
political stability becomes negative and insignificant. We read this
as: the GDP signal is genuine, the population-scaling signal is
diluted when the largest senders are removed (a few thousand of the
$\sim 18{,}000$ track athletes are concentrated in those 20 countries),
and the political-stability coefficient remains noise.

\textbf{Bartik shift-share.}\quad
We constructed a Bartik-style instrument from country shares across
nine track-event groups and leave-one-out aggregate event flows. The
first-stage $F$-statistic on the Bartik exposure as an instrument
for $\log\text{GDPpc}$ is 13.5, below the modern threshold of
$F > 104.7$ for valid 2SLS $t$-tests at the 5\% level
\citep{lee2022valid}, so we do not rely on the IV. As a covariate the
Bartik enters at $+0.95$ ($p<0.10$) and absorbs the GDP coefficient
(a mechanical collinearity result, since sport mix is correlated with
country wealth). The Bartik specification is reported in
\texttt{output/bartik\_results.txt} for transparency and is not load-
bearing in this paper.

\section{Discussion}
%========================================================================

Three substantive conclusions emerge from the country-level
analysis, and a fourth from what we tried but could not find.

First, \textbf{wealth and population scale govern entry into the
international NCAA pipeline}. Both $\log \text{GDPpc}$ and $\log
\text{pop}$ are large and statistically significant in the
extensive-margin probit; political stability is not. The mechanism
is the fixed-cost story familiar from migration economics: producing
a recruitable athlete requires some minimum infrastructure (clubs,
schools, transport, equipment, parental capacity) and the discovery
cost on the US side, both of which scale with national resource
flow. Below the corresponding income threshold, no athletes are
produced regardless of how well-governed the country is.

Second, \textbf{conditional on entry, the intensive margin is
dominated by sublinear population scaling and a small wealth gradient}.
The cohort-population elasticity of $0.33$ is the largest single
fact in the country panel: a doubling of source-country 15--24
population is associated with only $\sim 26\%$ more NCAA athletes,
not the proportional doubling that a constant-returns supply curve
would predict. This is consistent with diminishing-returns
recruitment at the country level: as a source country grows, the
marginal recruit is harder to find, the marginal coach has less
information, and the US side's bilateral recruiting capacity
saturates. The GDP-per-capita coefficient of $0.37$ adds a modest
wealth gradient on top: among countries of similar size, a richer
one produces about $\exp(0.37 \cdot \ln 2) - 1 \approx 29\%$ more
athletes per doubling of GDP per capita.

Third, \textbf{institutional quality and macro shocks do not have
detectable intensive-margin effects} once population scale is freely
estimated. Political stability, disaster events, disaster deaths,
conflict years, refugee outflows, CPI inflation, and terms-of-trade
shocks are all individually insignificant in the count model. A
previous draft of this work emphasised political stability as the
robust intensive-margin driver; we now show that result was an
artefact of imposing a unit population elasticity. The honest
conclusion is that we cannot detect institutional or shock effects
beyond the joint role of wealth and scale.

Fourth, and most importantly, \textbf{the country-level macro design
explains only about a fifth of the variation in athlete supply}
($R^2 \approx 0.21$ in the base count model, $0.24$ with the full
shock battery). More than three-quarters of the cross-country
variation in international NCAA track-and-field representation is
not explained by GDP, population, governance, conflict, disasters,
shocks, inflation, or refugees. We attribute the bulk of this
residual to country-specific recruitment pipelines: the
Kenyan-distance-running ecosystem centered on Iten, the MVP and
Racers Track Club networks that dominate Jamaican sprinting, similar
Caribbean conduits, the Norwegian and Australian middle-distance
scenes, the German and Polish throwing pipelines, and so on. These
are pipeline structures that macro covariates cannot capture, and a
complete model of international NCAA supply would require
sport-level network data we do not have. Our reading is that the
ceiling on what country-year macro variables can explain in this
domain is closer to $R^2 = 0.30$ than $0.60$.

\section{Caveats and limitations}
%========================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cross-section, not panel.} Roster snapshots are
2024--25 / 2025--26. Macro covariates are 2023 plus 2010--23
aggregates. A descriptive pseudo-panel constructed from athletes'
class-year (Fr/So/Jr/Sr/Gr) gives at most five entry cohorts. A
static DiD using country and year fixed effects with COVID-19
stringency interacted with the 2021--22 entry cohorts gives a small
positive coefficient ($+0.07$, $p = 0.06$, country-clustered SEs).
We report this in \texttt{output/event\_study.txt} as a descriptive
exercise rather than an identified event study: there is no clean
pre-period (the cohort 2020 is itself a COVID-affected cohort), and
the class-year-to-entry-year mapping is imputation-based.

\item \textbf{Coverage.} Hometown is observed for 87.6\% of track
athletes; the unobserved 12.4\% are missing not at random
(school-page rendering issues that systematically affect schools
with JS-rendered rosters).

\item \textbf{Track-and-field dominance.} 96\% of the international
sample competes in track. The paper's findings should be understood
as describing the international track-and-field recruitment market
specifically.

\item \textbf{Cohort denominator approximation.} We use the average
of the 15--24-year-old cohort 2018--2022 as the denominator. The
ideal denominator would be 18--22 to match enrolled ages; we
attempted this but the World Bank does not provide aggregate 18--22
totals (only 5-year bands as percentages of gender-specific totals).
Rescaling the 15--24 cohort by a constant fraction is observationally
equivalent in the count model (the constant is absorbed by the
intercept), so denominator choice does not change our slope
estimates, but a finer-grained cohort variable from national censuses
would be valuable.

\item \textbf{Athletes per capita is not athletic talent per capita.}
Recruiting selection, scholarship caps, language and visa frictions,
and US schools' bilateral relationships filter the population. We
observe the \emph{combined} effect of supply, selection, and demand.

\item \textbf{Bartik weak instrument.} The shift-share construction
on track-event composition has a first-stage $F = 13.5$, below the
modern $F > 104.7$ threshold \citep{lee2022valid} for valid 2SLS
$t$-tests at the 5\% level. We use it only as a transparency check,
not as identification.

\item \textbf{Modest $R^2$.} The residual 75--80\% of cross-country
variation in athlete supply is most plausibly driven by country-
specific recruitment pipelines that macro covariates cannot proxy.
\end{itemize}
